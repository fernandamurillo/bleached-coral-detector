Our Cause

Earth’s corals are increasingly at risk of suffering from coral bleaching due to climate change. The World Wildlife Fund stated that “According to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Association, between 2014 and 2017 around 75% of the world’s tropical coral reefs experienced heat-stress severe enough to trigger bleaching. For 30% of the world’s reefs, that heat-stress was enough to kill coral.” Since then, ocean temperatures have only risen, increasing the impact of coral bleaching on our environment. Join our cause and take action to save our reefs!

Why Coral Reefs Matter?

Coral reefs are the backbone of many ocean ecosystems, providing animals with shelter and food. According to the Coral Reef Alliance, “coral reefs are home to more than 25% of marine life”. Coral reefs support some of the most diverse ecosystems and can house thousands of different species in one reef alone. Without coral reefs, our ocean’s complex food web would be destroyed, and the ecosystem would collapse. Healthy coral reefs also benefit humans. In addition to providing jobs to thousands of people through a billion-dollar tourist industry, reefs also moderate stormy weather and waves for coastal communities. For these reasons, we must work to save these reefs from the many threats they face today.

Why Does Coral Bleaching Happen?

“Coral bleaching happens when corals lose their vibrant colors and turn white. But there’s a lot more to it than that. Coral are bright and colorful because of microscopic algae called zooxanthellae. The zooxanthellae live within the coral in a mutually beneficial relationship, each helping the other survive. But when the ocean environment changes—if it gets too hot, for instance—the coral stresses out and expels the algae. As the algae leaves, the coral fades until it looks like it’s been bleached. If the temperature stays high, the coral won’t let the algae back, and the coral will die.” - World Wildlife Fund (WWF)

How To Help

  • Volunteer in local beach/reef cleanups.
  • Pick up your trash when visiting the beach.
  • Use energy-efficient products to reduce your carbon footprint and greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Choose sustainable seafood to eat. (https://www.fishwatch.gov/)
  • Use sunscreens with chemicals that don’t harm marine life
  • Donate to the World Wide Fund for Nature, an International Organization that works to preserve wildlife and to reduce human impact on the environment (https://www.worldwildlife.org/initiatives)
  • Donate to the Coral Reef Alliance, a nonprofit which works to reduce threats on reefs, such as coral bleaching (https://coral.org/ )
